How Disconnector handles your data.

Mostly by not handling it. This document explains what Disconnector does on your phone, what permissions it uses, and what we do — and don't — collect.

Last updated: [DATE]

The short version

Disconnector runs entirely on your phone. There is no Disconnector server. There is no account. There is no login. We don't collect, store, or transmit any information about you, your device, or your behavior.

Disconnector uses Android's VPN system to cut internet for the apps you choose, but the "VPN" is local — it never connects out. Packets are dropped right on your device.

Everything you set up — which apps you've quieted, your schedules, your passcode — stays on your phone in Android's standard app storage. If you uninstall Disconnector, all of it is gone.

2. What information we collect

Nothing. Disconnector does not collect, store, transmit, or share any personal information.

What this means in practice

  • We do not have a server. There is no infrastructure that could collect data about you, even if we wanted to.
  • We do not have user accounts. You don't sign up. There is no email, username, or password to give us.
  • We do not use analytics or telemetry SDKs. There is no integration with Firebase Analytics, Google Analytics, Crashlytics, Mixpanel, or any similar service.
  • We do not use advertising SDKs. Disconnector contains no ads and no advertising frameworks.
  • We do not send crash reports off your device. If Disconnector crashes, you can report it via your normal Google Play feedback channel — that's between you and Google.
  • We do not track which apps you install, which apps you choose to quiet, when your schedules fire, how long you disconnect for, or anything else about how you use Disconnector.

3. Android permissions and what they do

Disconnector requests the following Android permissions. Each is used only for the purpose described, and none of these permissions cause any data to leave your device.

android.permission.BIND_VPN_SERVICE

Required to operate the per-app VPN that cuts the internet for selected apps. Granted by you via Android's "Connection request" consent dialog. The VPN never connects out — packets from the apps you've selected are dropped locally on your device.

FOREGROUND_SERVICE / FOREGROUND_SERVICE_SPECIAL_USE

Required by Android to keep the VPN service running while disconnects are active. Causes the persistent "Apps quieted" notification you see in your status bar — that notification is a system requirement for foreground services, not a tracking mechanism.

POST_NOTIFICATIONS

Used to display the persistent VPN-active notification and the optional "quiet hours started/ended" notifications when a schedule fires. You can deny this and Disconnector will still work; you'll just see less feedback.

SCHEDULE_EXACT_ALARM / USE_EXACT_ALARM

Required to fire your schedules at the exact times you set, even when your phone is idle. Used only to trigger your own configured schedules. Disconnector does not set any other alarms.

RECEIVE_BOOT_COMPLETED

Required to re-arm your schedule alarms after the phone reboots. Without this, your schedules would silently stop firing after every restart. Disconnector does not auto-start the VPN at boot — only the schedule alarms are restored.

QUERY_ALL_PACKAGES

Required to read the list of apps installed on your phone, so the Apps tab can show real apps with real icons and names rather than a generic catalog. The list of apps you have installed is read locally and never sent off your device.

USE_BIOMETRIC

Used only if you choose to enable biometric unlock for the optional in-app Lock feature. Biometric authentication is performed entirely by Android — Disconnector never sees or stores your fingerprint or face data. We only receive a yes/no result from the system.

4. The "VPN" — what it is and isn't

Disconnector uses Android's VpnService API because that's the only mechanism Android offers for per-app network control. This is the same API used by every legitimate VPN app, but Disconnector uses it differently.

What Disconnector's "VPN" does:

  • Marks the apps you've selected as routed through a local virtual network interface.
  • Reads the network packets those apps generate.
  • Discards them. That's the entire mechanism.

What Disconnector's "VPN" does not do:

  • It does not connect to any server, anywhere. There is no "tunnel" out.
  • It does not encrypt or proxy your traffic.
  • It does not inspect, log, store, or transmit your traffic.
  • It does not affect any apps other than the ones you've selected.
  • It does not affect any traffic when no disconnect is active.

If a fully offline phone could "feel" disconnected from the world, that's the closest analogy: Disconnector creates that experience for the specific apps you choose, while everything else continues to work normally.

5. Data stored on your device

Disconnector stores the following information on your phone, in Android's standard private app storage. This data is accessible only to Disconnector and is removed when you uninstall the app.

  • App selections. Which apps you've added to your quiet list.
  • Schedules. The recurring quiet windows you've configured (start time, end time, days of the week, name, on/off state).
  • Session history. Start and end timestamps of completed disconnect sessions, used to compute the weekly chart and lifetime hours on the Stats tab. Session durations are stored, not what you did during them.
  • Theme preference. Whether you've chosen Paper, Midnight, or System theme.
  • Onboarding state. A boolean flag indicating whether you've completed the initial setup, so we don't show it to you twice.
  • Lock settings. If you've enabled the optional in-app Lock, Disconnector stores your 4-digit passcode and your biometric-on-or-off preference. The passcode is currently stored as plain text in private app storage; we plan to move it into the Android Keystore in a future release.

None of this data is transmitted off your device.
